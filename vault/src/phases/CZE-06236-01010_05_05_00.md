### Evolutionary Timeline of the EDF Facility in Dukovany, Czech Republic

#### 1. Technology and Operations
- **March 2022**: The Czech government announced plans to launch a tender for a new unit at the Dukovany nuclear power plant to replace an existing unit. The facility currently operates four units, each with a capacity of 510 MW.
- **November 2023**: EDF submitted a bid to construct one EPR1200 reactor at Dukovany, with the potential for additional units as part of the tender. The project aims to add approximately 1,200 MW to the existing capacity.
- **February 2024**: Bids were submitted for a new fifth unit at Dukovany, with non-binding offers for three additional reactors. The construction start is planned for 2029, with trial operation expected in 2036.

#### 2. Investment and Development
- **March 2022**: The estimated cost for the new unit was reported to be around €6 billion ($6.39 billion), excluding inflation. This figure is expected to have risen due to project complexities.
- **November 2023**: The Czech government indicated that the project would likely exceed the initial cost estimates due to inflation and other factors.

#### 3. Project Status and Milestones
- **March 2022**: The Czech government announced the tender process for a new unit at Dukovany, with plans to finalize the supplier by 2024 and obtain construction permits by 2029.
- **November 2023**: CEZ Group confirmed the strong interest of EDF, KHNP, and Westinghouse in the project, with contracts expected to be signed in 2024.
- **February 2024**: The tender was expanded to include binding offers for units 5 and 6 at Dukovany and units 3 and 4 at the Temelin nuclear power plant. The government aimed to finalize contracts within a year.

#### 4. Labour
- **February 2024**: The Czech government emphasized the importance of localizing work with Czech suppliers as part of the bids, which could lead to job creation in the region, although specific job numbers were not mentioned.

#### 5. Ownership Structure
- **November 2023**: CEZ Group, which is 70% state-owned, is managing the tender process. The ownership structure of the new unit will depend on the final agreements made with the selected bidders, including EDF. The government is considering restructuring CEZ to enhance control over energy infrastructure, which may impact ownership dynamics.
