## DNK-08637-09839

### Evolutionary Timeline of the Ramboll Facility in North Sea Energy Island, Denmark

#### 1. Technology and Operations
- **June 2023**: Ramboll is contracted to assist in the development of the North Sea energy island, which will serve as a hub for gathering and distributing power from offshore wind farms. The facility is projected to have a capacity of 3 GW by 2033, sufficient to power approximately 3 million households, and aims to expand to 10 GW by 2040, enough to power 10 million homes in Northern Europe.

#### 2. Investment and Development
- **June 2023**: The total value of the project is reported to be DKK 225 million (approximately EUR 30 million). This investment is tied to the establishment of the artificial island’s electricity infrastructure, which is crucial for the green transition and the efficient transport of renewable energy.

#### 3. Project Status and Milestones
- **June 2023**: Ramboll announces its role as the client consultant for the Danish transmission system operator Energinet in the establishment of the North Sea energy island. This project is noted as one of the world’s first and largest offshore electrical infrastructure projects.

#### 4. Labour
- **June 2023**: The project is expected to employ 35 full-time Ramboll experts over the next 10 years, contributing to job creation in the renewable energy sector.

#### 5. Ownership Structure
- **June 2023**: Ramboll is involved as a consultant in the project, indicating that it does not own the facility exclusively. The project is being developed in collaboration with Energinet, the Danish transmission system operator, suggesting a partnership or joint venture structure rather than full ownership by Ramboll. 

This timeline encapsulates the key developments and milestones related to the Ramboll facility at the North Sea energy island, highlighting its significance in the renewable energy landscape of Denmark and Europe.

### Technology and Operations
- "By 2033, the island will have a capacity of 3 GW zero carbon electricity, equivalent to approximately half of Denmark’s electricity consumption today."
