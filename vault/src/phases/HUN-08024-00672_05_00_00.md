### Evolutionary Timeline of the Rosatom Facility in Paks II, Hungary

#### 1. Technology and Operations
- **August 2022**: The Hungarian Atomic Energy Authority (OAH) granted Rosatom a construction license for two Generation III+ VVER-1200 reactors at the Paks II nuclear power plant site. This facility is designed to enhance Hungary's energy sovereignty and is expected to operate for 60 years.
- **April 2023**: General construction work continued on auxiliary buildings and facilities at the Paks II site, with the process of obtaining license documentation for specific types of work and equipment manufacturing initiated.
- **September 2023**: Rosatom announced that the first concrete pour at the Paks II nuclear power plant is expected by early 2025. Excavation work for the foundations of units 5 and 6 is ongoing, with significant soil removal planned.

#### 2. Investment and Development
- **September 2023**: The total investment for the Paks II project is reported to be $13.6 billion (approximately Rbs1.31 trillion). Amendments to the construction and financing agreement were discussed in April 2023, although specific details were not disclosed.

#### 3. Project Status and Milestones
- **January 2014**: An intergovernmental agreement between Hungary and Russia was executed to build the Paks II nuclear power plant.
- **August 2022**: The construction license for the new power units was issued by the OAH.
- **April 2023**: Amendments to the construction and financing agreement were agreed upon, with ongoing construction work at the site.
- **September 2023**: Rosatom confirmed that the main stage of construction began in August 2023, with plans for the first concrete pour by early 2025.

#### 4. Labour
- **September 2023**: It was reported that up to 1,000 jobs could be created at the Paks II nuclear power plant by 2024, with expectations of increasing to 10,000 jobs during the peak construction phase.

#### 5. Ownership Structure
- The facility is part of a joint venture between Hungary and Russia, with Rosatom being the primary contractor and supplier for the project. The project is supported by a Russian state loan, indicating a collaborative ownership structure rather than exclusive ownership by Rosatom.
